Sentence Structure & Word Order (Satzbau und Wortstellung)
===========================================================

🧠 General Definition

German word order follows specific rules about where the verb and other parts of the sentence go.
The position of the verb tells you what kind of sentence it is — statement, question, command, or subordinate clause.

German: Satzbau und Wortstellung
Pronunciation (English): Zahts-bow oont vort-shtel-loong
English meaning: Sentence structure and word order

1️⃣ Main Clauses – Verb-Second Rule (Hauptsatz – Verbzweitstellung)
🧠 Definition

In a main clause, the verb is always in the second position — even if another word comes first.
The first position can be the subject or another element (like time or place), but the verb stays second.

🧩 Examples

Ich gehe heute ins Kino.
(Eekh gay-uh hoy-tuh ins kee-no)
→ I’m going to the cinema today.

Heute gehe ich ins Kino.
(Hoy-tuh gay-uh eekh ins kee-no)
→ Today, I’m going to the cinema.

Mein Freund spielt Fußball.
(Mine froynt shpeelt foos-bahl)
→ My friend plays football.

Morgen arbeite ich zu Hause.
(Mor-gen ar-bye-tuh eekh tsoo how-zeh)
→ Tomorrow, I work from home.

Er liest ein Buch.
(Air leest ine bookh)
→ He is reading a book.

🧩 Tip:
Whatever comes first, the verb must always be second.
(E.g., Time → Verb → Subject → Rest)

2️⃣ Subordinate Clauses – Verb-Final (Nebensatz – Verbletztstellung)
🧠 Definition

In a subordinate clause (introduced by subordinating conjunctions like weil, dass, wenn), the verb goes to the end of the clause.

🧩 Examples

Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil es regnet.
(Eekh bly-buh tsoo how-zeh, vyle es rayg-net)
→ I’m staying home because it’s raining.

Er sagt, dass er müde ist.
(Air zahkt, dass air myoo-duh ist)
→ He says that he is tired.

Wenn ich Zeit habe, gehe ich spazieren.
(Ven eekh tsyt hah-buh, gay-uh eekh shpah-tsee-ren)
→ When I have time, I go for a walk.

Ich weiß nicht, ob er kommt.
(Eekh vys nikht, ob air komt)
→ I don’t know if he’s coming.

Bevor ich esse, wasche ich meine Hände.
(Beh-for eekh es-seh, vah-sheh eekh my-nuh hen-duh)
→ Before I eat, I wash my hands.

🧩 Tip:
Main clause → verb 2nd
Subordinate clause → verb at the end.

3️⃣ Questions (Fragen)
🟢 a) Yes/No Questions (Ja/Nein-Fragen)

The verb comes first, followed by the subject.

Kommst du heute? (Komst doo hoy-tuh) → Are you coming today?

Hast du Hunger? (Hahst doo hoong-er) → Are you hungry?

Geht er zur Arbeit? (Gayt air tsoor ar-byt) → Is he going to work?

Magst du Kaffee? (Mahkst doo kah-fay) → Do you like coffee?

Wohnst du hier? (Vohnst doo heer) → Do you live here?

🟣 b) W-Questions (W-Fragen)

Start with a question word like wer, was, wo, wann, warum, etc.
The verb comes second.

Wie heißt du? (Vee hyst doo) → What’s your name?

Wo wohnst du? (Voh vohnst doo) → Where do you live?

Wann kommst du? (Vahn komst doo) → When are you coming?

Warum lernst du Deutsch? (Vah-room lernst doo doytch) → Why are you learning German?

Was machst du heute? (Vahs makhst doo hoy-tuh) → What are you doing today?

4️⃣ Commands (Imperatives)
🧠 Definition

Used to give orders or requests.
The verb comes first, and the subject (you) is usually dropped.

🧩 Examples

Komm hierher! (Kom heer-hair) → Come here!

Mach das Fenster zu! (Mahkh dahs fen-ster tsoo) → Close the window!

Sei ruhig! (Zye roo-ikh) → Be quiet!

Lies das Buch! (Lees dahs bookh) → Read the book!

Warte einen Moment! (Var-tuh eye-nen mo-ment) → Wait a moment!

🧩 Tip:

For “du” → verb stem only (Komm!)

For “ihr” → verb + “t” (Kommt!)

For “Sie” → full form (Kommen Sie!)

5️⃣ Inversion Rules (Inversion – Umstellung)
🧠 Definition

When something other than the subject (like time, place, or an adverb) starts the sentence, the verb comes second, and the subject moves after it.

🧩 Examples

Heute gehe ich ins Büro. → Today I go to the office.

Morgen fahre ich nach Berlin. → Tomorrow I travel to Berlin.

Am Abend lese ich ein Buch. → In the evening I read a book.

Dann essen wir. → Then we eat.

Plötzlich regnete es. → Suddenly it rained.

🧩 Tip:
Inversion doesn’t change the meaning — it’s just for emphasis or flow.

6️⃣ Negation (Verneinung)
🧠 Definition

In German, you can say “no” or “not” using nicht or kein, depending on what you are negating.

🧩 Rules

nicht → used to negate verbs, adjectives, adverbs, or whole sentences

kein → used to negate nouns that have no article or an indefinite article (ein)

💬 Examples with “nicht”

Ich komme nicht. → I’m not coming.

Er ist nicht hier. → He’s not here.

Das ist nicht gut. → That’s not good.

Ich mag das nicht. → I don’t like that.

Wir spielen nicht heute. → We’re not playing today.

💬 Examples with “kein”

Ich habe kein Geld. → I have no money.

Sie hat keine Zeit. → She has no time.

Wir sehen keinen Film. → We’re not watching a movie.

Er hat kein Auto. → He has no car.

Ich trinke keinen Kaffee. → I drink no coffee / I don’t drink coffee.

🧩 Tip:

“kein” replaces “ein”

“nicht” negates everything else
👉 Ich habe ein Auto → Ich habe kein Auto
👉 Ich bin müde → Ich bin nicht müde

⚖️ Rules & Exceptions (Regeln und Ausnahmen)

Verb-second rule always applies in main clauses — no exceptions.

Verb-final rule applies in subordinate clauses (introduced by weil, dass, obwohl).

Inversion doesn’t change meaning — only emphasis.

Nicht goes before adjectives/adverbs but after the verb.

Kein changes with gender and case (kein, keine, keinen, etc.).

✅ Summary
